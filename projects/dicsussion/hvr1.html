1. The Science of Scarcity

Geochemistry

The Lanthanide Contraction

REEs are not "rare" in abundance but rare in concentrated deposits. Their chemical similarity makes separation difficult.

  • Physics: Progressive filling of the 4f electron subshell.
  • Consequence: Steady decrease in ionic radius from Lanthanum (La) to Lutetium (Lu).
  • Processing Impact: Nearly identical chemical behavior necessitates hundreds of solvent extraction stages.

Carbonatites

Igneous rocks (>50% carbonate). High grade LREE. Main source of global supply.

Ion-Adsorption Clays

Weathered granites. Low grade but easy to leach. Primary source of HREEs.

Insight: Production vs. Reserves

While Vietnam and Brazil hold ~33% of global reserves combined, they contribute <2% of production. The bottleneck is not geology, but processing capacity.

The Myanmar Factor

Myanmar produces 38,000 tons, almost exclusively HREEs from clays, acting as a raw material vassal state for Chinese refiners.

With European markets closed, logistics have pivoted to the Trans-Siberian corridor and Far East ports.

Bottleneck: Zabaikalsk

The primary rail crossing faces delays of 9-20 days due to platform shortages and gauge changing.

Container Imbalance

Trade deficit with China leaves containers piling up in Moscow, creating shortages in the Urals for export.

7. India: Importers & Dependency

"China Plus One"

India holds massive reserves (Monazite) but lacks magnet manufacturing. ~90% of magnets are imported. Govt launched ₹7,280 Cr PLI scheme to fix this.

SME Sector Manufacturing Sintered NdFeB blocks for pumps, motors, generators. 90% dependency on China.

Strategic Shift: The PLI Scheme

India is pivoting from a "Buy" to "Make" strategy. By partnering with Australia (Lynas) for intermediate oxides and incentivizing companies like Sona Comstar to build magnet plants, India aims to break the "Mine-to-Magnet" Chinese monopoly.
