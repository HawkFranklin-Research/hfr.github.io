Introduction: GenAI in Healthcare

The integration of Generative AI into healthcare represents a technological inflection point with the potential to revolutionize clinical practice, biomedical research, and patient care. This high-stakes domain demands unprecedented levels of scrutiny regarding trustworthiness, safety, and policy compliance.

The emergence of specialized academic venues like the GenAI4Health workshop at NeurIPS underscores the research community's recognition of these challenges.

Structural Changes

  • Dedicated Ethics Review Chairs
  • Requirement for impact statements
  • Formation of ethics review committees
  • Specialized tracks (Datasets, Benchmarks, Position Papers)

Key Insight: These structural changes create "pull" factors that shape the research agenda, moving ethics from a peripheral concern to a core component of AI research.

The Six Pillars of Trustworthy AI

Human Agency & Oversight

Maintaining meaningful human control, with emerging research on human-AI co-evolution and RLHF for alignment.

Fairness & Non-Discrimination

Algorithms for group fairness, certified fairness guarantees, and bias mitigation in medical applications.

Transparency & Explainability

Model-agnostic explanations (SHAP, LIME), interpretable ML, and causal inference methods.

Robustness & Accuracy

Adversarial defenses, OOD generalization, and uncertainty quantification for clinical reliability.

Privacy & Security

Differential privacy, federated learning, and secure multi-party computation for health data.

Accountability

Audit trails, model monitoring, and governance frameworks across the AI lifecycle.

These foundational papers establish the conceptual framework for trustworthy AI in healthcare, bridging technical and policy perspectives.

Benchmarking Trust in Healthcare AI

The CARES Benchmark

CARES (Comprehensive Benchmark of Trustworthiness in Medical Vision Language Models) evaluates Med-LVLMs across five dimensions:

  • Trustfulness: Factuality and uncertainty awareness
  • Fairness: Performance disparities across demographics
  • Safety: Jailbreaking and toxicity vulnerabilities
  • Privacy: Information leakage risks
  • Robustness: Performance under distribution shifts

Findings: Current Med-LVLMs exhibit significant trustworthiness concerns across all dimensions.

Evaluation Paradigm Shift

The evolution from performance-centric to process-centric evaluation in medical AI.

Human-in-the-Loop Approaches

Pathologist-in-the-Loop Framework

"Aligning Synthetic Medical Images with Clinical Knowledge using Human Feedback" (NeurIPS 2023) introduces RLHF to finetune diffusion models:

  1. Expert pathologist provides feedback on clinical plausibility
  2. Train reward model from human feedback
  3. Reward model guides diffusion model generation

Result: Dramatic improvement in quality, utility, and expert-rated plausibility of synthetic medical images.

Construct Validity Critique

A position paper from GenAI4Health challenges medical LLM benchmarks:

  • High performance on medical exams ≠ clinical competence
  • Exams designed for humans may not assess LLMs appropriately
  • Need for process-oriented evaluation beyond output metrics

Implication: Pushes field toward interactive, process-centric evaluation.

Risk Management Case Studies

Demographic Bias

CARES benchmark findings:

  • Performance drops for elderly patients
  • Racial disparities in accuracy
  • Imbalanced training data as root cause

Safety Vulnerabilities

Key failure modes identified:

  • Jailbreaking prompts bypass safety filters
  • Overcautiousness limiting utility
  • Confident but incorrect OOD responses

Privacy Risks

Privacy-enhancing technologies (PETs):

  • Differentially private training
  • Information leakage defenses
  • Privacy-utility tradeoff management

Key Insight: These case studies reveal that technical solutions alone are insufficient - robust risk management requires interdisciplinary approaches combining technical, clinical, and policy perspectives.

Regulatory Landscape for AI in Health

European Union

  • EU AI Act: Risk-based framework with stringent requirements for high-risk healthcare AI
  • Medical Device Regulation (MDR): Rule 11 specifically addresses Software as a Medical Device (SaMD)
  • Complex interplay between general AI Act and sector-specific MDR

United States

  • FDA: AI/ML SaMD Action Plan, Predetermined Change Control Plans (PCCPs)
  • HIPAA: Strict rules for protected health information (PHI)
  • FTC: Enforcement against unfair/deceptive practices in consumer health apps

Challenge: Regulatory frameworks struggle to keep pace with exponential AI advancement (e.g., EU AI Act proposed pre-ChatGPT, amended late in process).

Policy and Compliance Case Studies

Classifying GenAI under EU MDR

Position paper analyzes how GenAI tools should be classified under the EU's Medical Device Regulation (Rule 11):

  • Classification determines regulatory pathway (Class IIa, IIb, III)
  • Risk of under-regulation (safety) vs over-regulation (innovation)
  • Provides framework for developers and regulators

Key question: Should synthetic data generators be classified as medical devices?

Open vs. Closed LLMs in Healthcare

Debate between open-source (LLaMA) and closed models (GPT-4) for healthcare:

Open Models: Transparency, adaptability, auditability

Closed Models: Performance, centralized support

Trend: Growing interest in open models for specialized medical adaptation

The Fairness vs. Anti-Discrimination Disconnect

Technical Fairness Paradigm

  • Consequentialist: Focused on statistical parity in outcomes
  • Metrics: Equalized odds, demographic parity, etc.
  • Context-blind: Same metrics applied across domains
  • Output-focused: Evaluates final decisions/predictions

Legal Anti-Discrimination Paradigm

  • Procedural: Focused on individual rights and fair processes
  • Doctrines: Disparate treatment, disparate impact
  • Context-aware: Considers domain-specific norms
  • Process-focused: Examines decision-making pathways

Critical Gap: A system can be "fair" by technical metrics while still being legally discriminatory. This is particularly dangerous in healthcare where subtle biases in language or emphasis can influence clinical decisions.

Identified Research Gaps

Legally-Grounded Discrimination Testing

Current fairness metrics fail to align with legal anti-discrimination principles. Need for:

  • Adversarial legal reviews based on case law
  • Procedural auditing frameworks
  • Representational harm detection

Post-Market Surveillance

EU AI Act and FDA emphasize monitoring but lack technical specifications:

  • Real-time model drift detection
  • Safe update validation protocols
  • Adverse event reporting systems

Open-Source Governance

Models for safely leveraging open-source LLMs:

  • Consortium-based validation
  • Shared responsibility models
  • Public-private partnerships

Human Oversight Standardization

"Human-in-the-loop" needs concrete implementation:

  • Taxonomy of oversight levels
  • Technical requirements per level
  • Clinician competency frameworks

Conclusion

This review reveals a field in rapid maturation, grappling with the profound responsibilities of deploying Generative AI in healthcare. The discourse has evolved from abstract principles to concrete evaluation tools and process-centric alignment.

Institutional changes in AI conferences have embedded ethics as a core research concern
Technical research is increasingly engaging with regulatory frameworks
Critical gaps remain, particularly around fairness testing and governance

Recommendations for GenAI4Health 2025

Research Priorities

Focus on legally-grounded discrimination testing frameworks and robust post-market surveillance mechanisms for adaptive AI systems in clinical settings.

Community Building

Strengthen interdisciplinary collaboration between AI researchers, clinicians, legal scholars, and policymakers to address complex socio-technical challenges.
